# /aha-development — Aleut Federal Safety

> Aleut Federal company context lives in the **aleut-federal-context** Skill (auto-invoked for AF-related questions).

Develop an **Activity Hazard Analysis (AHA)** for a specific construction activity. AHAs are required by EM 385-1-1 and are the primary control mechanism for activity-specific safety on federal construction.

## Aleut Federal Context — When an AHA Is Required

- **EM 385-1-1 Section 01.A** requires an AHA for each major work activity prior to starting that activity on a USACE / NAVFAC construction site.
- **OSHA 29 C.F.R. 1926.21(b)(2)** requires instruction in the recognition and avoidance of unsafe conditions and the regulations applicable to the work environment.
- The AHA is reviewed and accepted by the SSHO and approved by the AF Site Superintendent **before** work begins.
- On USACE sites, the Government QAR / safety office reviews and accepts AHAs before NTP.

A specific AHA is required for any activity with notable hazard, including:

- Work at heights (above 6 ft per OSHA; lower per EM 385-1-1).
- Excavation > 5 ft.
- Confined space entry.
- Hot work / welding / cutting.
- Crane and rigging.
- Demolition.
- Electrical work.
- Energized work / lockout-tagout.
- Hazmat handling (lead, asbestos, silica, beryllium).
- Diving / marine.
- Heavy equipment operation.
- Material handling / suspended loads.
- Work involving live utilities.
- Night work / low visibility.

## AHA Structure

The AHA captures, for each Principal Step in the activity:

1. **Step** — clear, specific (e.g., "Set up shoring before excavation enters > 5 ft", not "Excavate").
2. **Hazards** — every credible hazard associated with the step (struck-by, caught-in, fall, electrical, chemical, biological, thermal, ergonomic, etc.).
3. **Controls** — engineering, administrative, PPE; hierarchy of controls in order (eliminate → substitute → engineering → administrative → PPE).
4. **Inspection requirements** — pre-task and during-task inspections; checklist references.
5. **Training requirements** — who is qualified to do the step (specific OSHA / EM 385-1-1 / agency-specific training).
6. **Equipment to be used** — including PPE.
7. **References** — OSHA citation, EM 385-1-1 section, manufacturer's instructions.

A standard AHA form (USACE provides one; many programs use ENG Form 5085 or a similar layout) holds all of this in tabular form.

## Workflow

### Step 1: Identify and Bound the Activity

The activity should be discrete enough to analyze meaningfully but not so granular every nail-driving needs its own AHA. Guidance:

- One AHA per **distinct procedure with distinct hazards** (e.g., "Concrete formwork" vs "Concrete placement" vs "Concrete finishing" may each need their own).
- One AHA per **high-hazard activity** even if short duration (e.g., a single crane pick).
- Master AHAs for repeated routine activities (general site work) with task-specific AHAs as overlays.

### Step 2: Walk the Activity Mentally

Break the activity into principal steps in execution order. Typical 5–15 steps.

### Step 3: Identify Hazards per Step

Use the hierarchy of hazard categories. Don't stop at the obvious — surface secondary hazards (e.g., a step has a noise hazard even if the headline hazard is fall).

### Step 4: Apply Controls in Hierarchy Order

Start with **eliminate** — can we avoid the hazard entirely (e.g., prefab off-site to reduce on-site work at height)? Then **substitute** — less hazardous material. Then **engineering** — guardrails, shielding, ventilation. Then **administrative** — work order, training, signs. Then **PPE** — last line.

### Step 5: Verify Training and Qualifications

For each step, identify the qualifications required to do it safely. Cross-reference to the project's training matrix. If anyone slated to do this step lacks the qualification, fix that before work starts.

### Step 6: Inspection Requirements

- **Pre-task** — equipment inspections, weather check, atmospheric tests, PPE inspection, area clear.
- **During-task** — observed by competent person, hold points.
- **Post-task** — clean-up, lessons learned.

### Step 7: Review and Approve

- **SSHO accepts** the AHA.
- **Site Superintendent approves**.
- **Government QAR / safety** reviews on USACE sites.
- **All workers** doing the activity sign that they've been briefed before the activity starts.

### Step 8: Daily Use and Update

The AHA is **briefed daily** at the toolbox talk before the activity starts. Update when:

- A near-miss reveals a missing hazard or control.
- Conditions change (weather, scope, equipment).
- A new worker joins the crew (re-brief them).

## Common AHA Examples — Quick References

| Activity | Key hazards | Key references |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Excavation > 5 ft | Cave-in, atmospheric, struck-by | OSHA 1926 Subpart P; EM 385-1-1 Sec 25 |
| Fall-protected work > 6 ft | Fall, falling object | OSHA 1926 Subpart M; EM 385-1-1 Sec 21 |
| Confined space | Atmospheric, engulfment, entrapment | OSHA 1926 Subpart AA; EM 385-1-1 Sec 34 |
| Hot work | Fire, eye, fume | OSHA 1926 Subpart J; EM 385-1-1 Sec 10 |
| Crane lift | Struck-by, rigging failure, overhead | OSHA 1926 Subpart CC; EM 385-1-1 Sec 16 |
| Demolition | Collapse, struck-by, hazmat | OSHA 1926 Subpart T; EM 385-1-1 Sec 23 |
| Electrical work | Shock, arc flash | OSHA 1926 Subpart K; NFPA 70E; EM 385-1-1 Sec 11 |
| Heavy equipment | Struck-by, rollover, ergo | OSHA 1926 Subpart O; EM 385-1-1 Sec 18 |

## Hard Rules

- **No work without an approved AHA** for activities requiring one.
- **No worker on the activity without daily briefing** signed.
- **SSHO can stop work** if conditions deviate from the AHA; don't override.
- **Update on incident or near-miss** — the AHA is wrong if a hazard materialized that wasn't in it.
- **Subcontractor AHAs** — subs follow our AHA or submit their own for the activity, accepted by SSHO before work.
